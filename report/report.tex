\section{Introduction}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The short-term rental market has expanded substantially, with platforms such as
Airbnb hosting millions of listings worldwide. Predicting listing demand is
commercially important for hosts, property managers, and platform operators.
Existing work on Airbnb demand prediction largely relies on structured attributes
such as price, location, and review scores \cite{rezazadeh2021}. However,
listings similar in price and review scores often differ in the quality and tone
of their host profiles and text descriptions.

\textbf{Research question:} \textit{Does NLP-derived text from host profiles and
guest reviews improve demand prediction beyond base listing features alone, and
do semantic embeddings add further value?} We design a controlled ablation where
three classifiers at different complexity tiers are evaluated on base-only versus
base plus NLP-engineered text features, with an additional SBERT PCA embedding
variant for FT-Transformer.

\textbf{Dataset:} We use publicly available data from Inside Airbnb
(\url{https://insideairbnb.com}) \cite{insideairbnb}, covering eight Australian
cities (Brisbane, Melbourne, Sunshine Coast, Mornington Peninsula, Northern
Rivers, Barossa Valley, Barwon South West, Mid-North Coast) across eight monthly
snapshots (May~2025--January~2026). The training set comprises 115{,}961 listing
observations; the dataset is predominantly cross-sectional as most cities
contribute a single snapshot, while Brisbane, Sunshine Coast, and Mid-North Coast
contribute four to six snapshots across May--November 2025. Sydney, Tasmania, and
Western Australia (31{,}675 listings) are withheld as a geographic
generalisation split.

%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Literature Review}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Rezazadeh Kalehbasti et al.\ \cite{rezazadeh2021} benchmark machine learning
models for Airbnb price prediction, finding gradient-boosted trees consistently
outperform linear models on structured tabular features. Their work excludes
unstructured text, motivating our NLP ablation design.

Hutto and Gilbert \cite{hutto2014} introduced VADER, a lexicon-based sentiment
analyser calibrated for informal text. We apply VADER to host profiles, listing
descriptions, and guest reviews to extract compound, positive, negative, and
neutral sentiment scores, providing fast text feature engineering without
requiring neural embeddings.

Prokhorenkova et al.\ \cite{prokhorenkova2018} introduced CatBoost, demonstrating
that \textit{ordered boosting} eliminates prediction-shift bias in standard
gradient boosting by computing residuals on a permutation-ordered subset of
training data. This is our medium-complexity baseline and the qualifying
algorithm (NeurIPS 2018).

Gorishniy et al.\ \cite{gorishniy2021} propose the Feature Tokenizer +
Transformer (FT-Transformer), showing Transformer architectures match or exceed
gradient-boosted trees on several tabular benchmarks when each feature is first
embedded via a learned per-feature linear map (NeurIPS 2021). Notably, the paper
acknowledges that tree-based models remain competitive; our results corroborate
this finding on a real-world demand prediction task.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Feature Construction and Preprocessing}

Raw listings from Inside Airbnb provide structured attributes and free-text
fields. We engineer 97 base and NLP features plus 32 SBERT PCA components (total 129 features)
\cite{reimers2019sbert} for the embedding ablation (Table~\ref{tab:features}).
All statistics used in feature construction are computed only on the training
portion of each outer fold to prevent leakage.

\midrule

  bathrooms); minimum nights; host tenure, response rate, superhost status,
  identity verification, instant bookability; review scores; amenity group
  scores; location features (distance from city centroid). \\[2pt]

  type-token ratio; presence indicator. \\[2pt]

  Same eleven NLP features applied to the \texttt{host\_about} text field. \\[2pt]
Review NLP      & 11 &
  VADER compound mean, std, min, max across all listing reviews; review count
  before snapshot cutoff; Flesch-Kincaid mean; recency-weighted sentiment;
  positive, negative, and neutral means. \\

  review-text SBERT embeddings, fitted on the training split only. \\

\textbf{Target construction.}
The demand target is binary: a listing is labelled high-demand if its
unavailability rate $r = (90 - \texttt{availability\_90})/90$ exceeds the
75th percentile computed on the training fold (threshold $= 0.733$, positive-class
rate $= 0.25$). We exclude \texttt{estimated\_occupancy\_l365d}: Inside Airbnb
computes it from review counts (Pearson $r = 0.638$ with
\texttt{number\_of\_reviews\_ltm}), creating feature--target leakage when review
NLP features are present. Long-term rentals (\texttt{minimum\_nights} $> 90$)
and never-reviewed listings (\texttt{number\_of\_reviews} $= 0$) are removed
before splitting. Missing numeric values are replaced with per-column medians
computed on each outer training fold.

\textbf{Logistic Regression (simple).}
L2-penalised logistic regression optimises the cross-entropy loss, yielding a
linear decision boundary with feature standardisation applied internally.
$C = 0.01$ was selected in all 10 outer folds.

\textbf{CatBoost (medium, NeurIPS 2018).}
Gradient-boosted symmetric trees with ordered boosting \cite{prokhorenkova2018},
which eliminates prediction-shift bias by computing residuals on a
permutation-ordered training subset. Tree depth $= 15$ was selected in all 10
folds. Fixed: 100 iterations, learning rate $= 0.1$.

\textbf{FT-Transformer (complex, NeurIPS 2021).}
Each feature is tokenised via $\mathbf{t}_i = x_i\mathbf{W}_i + \mathbf{b}_i$
\cite{gorishniy2021}. A \texttt{[CLS]} token is prepended and processed by
$L$ Pre-LayerNorm Transformer layers (GELU, multi-head self-attention), enabling
all-pair feature interactions. Implemented from scratch in PyTorch on a G4 GPU
(${\approx}400$s/fold). Fixed: $d{=}32$, 4 heads, ffn dim$=128$, 15 epochs,
patience 5.

\subsection{Cross-Validation and Hyperparameter Tuning}

\textbf{Outer CV.} 10-fold cross-validation ($k = 10$), implemented from scratch.
All models use identical folds. The demand-target threshold is recomputed on
each outer training fold to prevent distributional leakage.

\textbf{Inner CV.} Within each outer training fold, hyperparameters are selected
by nested 3-fold CV (also from scratch) using macro-F1 as the inner criterion.
Grid search covers all candidates in Table~\ref{tab:hpgrid}. Logistic and
CatBoost were tuned on 115{,}961 training rows; FT-Transformer was tuned on a
53{,}348-row subset due to GPU runtime constraints.

\textbf{Model complexity and CV performance.}
CatBoost (AUC $= 0.903$) outperforms both Logistic Regression
($0.792$, $\Delta = 0.111$) and FT-Transformer ($0.839$, $\Delta = 0.064$).
The large gap over Logistic Regression confirms that short-stay demand is
governed by strong non-linear interactions between price, location, capacity,
and review scores that gradient-boosted trees capture far more effectively
than a linear boundary. The confusion matrix (Table~\ref{tab:cm}) reveals a
precision--recall trade-off: CatBoost achieves high specificity ($0.940$) but
moderate recall ($0.720$), correctly identifying 70\% of high-demand listings
while producing few false positives. FT-Transformer outperforms Logistic Regression ($0.839$ vs $0.792$) but
falls below CatBoost, suggesting attention-based interactions help yet cannot
replicate axis-aligned tree splits over structured demand signals. The held-out test (Table~\ref{tab:test})
provides the primary cross-model comparison. Its encoder-layer count showed no
consistent winner, while all configurations selected learning rate $0.001$,
suggesting depth-insensitivity on this cross-sectional tabular task.

\textbf{Hyperparameter selection.}
Logistic Regression converged to $C = 0.01$ and CatBoost to depth $= 15$ in all
10 outer folds with both the largest candidates tested in their respective grids
$\{0.0001, 0.001, 0.01\}$ and $\{9, 12, 15\}$. This reflects a well-known
statistical relationship: with 115{,}961 training instances, models require less
regularisation and benefit from greater capacity than smaller datasets, as the
larger sample provides sufficient implicit regularisation against overfitting.
This finding suggests grids should be extended upward for large-scale tabular
datasets in future work.

\textbf{Temporal and geographic generalisation.}
The predominantly cross-sectional training data means CV folds reflect within-city
variation, so the held-out test and geographic generalisation are the primary
performance measures. All models show a substantial CV-to-test AUC drop
(CatBoost: $0.903{\to}0.806$; FT-Transformer: $0.839{\to}0.784$;
Logistic: $0.792{\to}0.753$), reflecting seasonal distribution shift to January
2026. Geographic generalisation to Sydney, Tasmania, and Western Australia
incurs a further drop (CatBoost: $0.806{\to}0.739$; FT-Transformer:
$0.784{\to}0.747$; Logistic: $0.753{\to}0.714$), reflecting city-specific
patterns absent during training. CatBoost consistently outperforms the other
models across all sets.

\textbf{Effect of NLP-engineered text features.}
NLP features provide minimal CV benefit ($\Delta\text{AUC} = +0.003$ Logistic,
$+0.001$ CatBoost, $-0.002$ FT-Transformer) but a small consistent gain on
held-out data: CatBoost $+0.008$/$+0.003$ (test/gen), Logistic $+0.002$/$+0.001$,
FT-Transformer $+0.006$/$+0.001$. SBERT PCA embeddings hurt FT-Transformer
($0.790\to0.783$ test AUC), suggesting PCA-compressed embeddings add noise when
simple engineered text features are already present.

\textbf{Alternatives considered.}
TabNet \cite{arik2021} was rejected (AAAI is not a qualifying venue). LightGBM
was rejected for sharing the same model class as CatBoost.
\texttt{estimated\_occupancy\_l365d} was rejected as a target due to
feature--target leakage ($r = 0.638$ with review counts).

%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This study compared Logistic Regression, CatBoost (NeurIPS 2018), and
FT-Transformer (NeurIPS 2021) for predicting high-demand Airbnb listings from
up to 129 engineered features (97 base and NLP, plus 32 SBERT PCA) across 115{,}961 predominantly cross-sectional Australian 
listing observations, with and without NLP-derived text and SBERT PCA embeddings resulting in three takeaway findings.  First, CatBoost achieves the best CV performance
(AUC $= 0.903$), outperforming Logistic Regression by $\Delta = 0.111$ and
FT-Transformer by $\Delta = 0.064$, confirming that demand is governed by
non-linear structural interactions best captured by gradient-boosted trees.
Second, all models suffer temporal and geographic distribution shift, with AUC
dropping by $0.097$ (CatBoost), $0.055$ (FT-Transformer), and $0.039$ (Logistic)
on the January 2026 test set; the cross-sectional training data means CV is
optimistic relative to true generalisation, making the held-out test the primary
evaluation. Third, engineered text features show negligible CV benefit but a
small consistent positive effect under distribution shift, whereas SBERT PCA
embeddings do not improve FT-Transformer performance in this experiment.
