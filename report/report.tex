\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The short-term rental market has expanded substantially, with platforms such as
Airbnb hosting millions of listings worldwide. Predicting listing demand is
commercially important for hosts, property managers, and platform operators.
Existing work on Airbnb demand prediction largely relies on structured attributes
such as price, location, and review scores \cite{rezazadeh2021}. However,
listings similar in price and review scores often differ in the quality and tone
of their host profiles and text descriptions --- differences that structured
features cannot directly capture.

\textbf{Research question:} \textit{Does NLP-derived text from host profiles and
guest reviews improve demand prediction beyond base listing features alone?}
This goes beyond the obvious prediction task: rather than simply predicting
availability from price and location, we design a controlled ablation where three
classifiers at different complexity tiers are each evaluated on two feature sets
--- base-only versus base plus NLP-engineered text features --- isolating the
marginal value of unstructured text.

\textbf{Dataset:} We use publicly available data from Inside Airbnb
(\url{https://insideairbnb.com}) \cite{insideairbnb}, covering eight Australian
cities (Brisbane, Melbourne, Sunshine Coast, Mornington Peninsula, Northern
Rivers, Barossa Valley, Barwon South West, Mid-North Coast) across eight monthly
snapshots (May~2025--January~2026). After filtering inactive and long-term
listings (Section~3.1), the training set comprises 53{,}348 listings.
Sydney, Tasmania, and Western Australia (31{,}675 listings) are withheld as a
geographic generalisation split.

%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Literature Review}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Rezazadeh Kalehbasti et al.\ \cite{rezazadeh2021} benchmark machine learning
models for Airbnb price prediction, finding gradient-boosted trees consistently
outperform linear models on structured tabular features. Their work excludes
unstructured text, motivating our NLP ablation design.

Hutto and Gilbert \cite{hutto2014} introduced VADER, a lexicon-based sentiment
analyser calibrated for informal text. We apply VADER to host profiles, listing
descriptions, and guest reviews to extract compound, positive, negative, and
neutral sentiment scores, providing fast text feature engineering without
requiring neural embeddings.

Prokhorenkova et al.\ \cite{prokhorenkova2018} introduced CatBoost, demonstrating
that \textit{ordered boosting} eliminates prediction-shift bias in standard
gradient boosting by computing residuals on a permutation-ordered subset of
training data. This is our medium-complexity baseline and the qualifying
algorithm (NeurIPS 2018).

Gorishniy et al.\ \cite{gorishniy2021} propose the Feature Tokenizer +
Transformer (FT-Transformer), showing Transformer architectures match or exceed
gradient-boosted trees on several tabular benchmarks when each feature is first
embedded via a learned per-feature linear map (NeurIPS 2021). Notably, the paper
acknowledges that tree-based models remain competitive; our results corroborate
this finding on a real-world demand prediction task.

\subsection{Feature Construction and Preprocessing}

Raw listings from Inside Airbnb provide structured attributes and free-text
fields. We engineer 97 features across four groups (Table~\ref{tab:features}).
All statistics used in feature construction are computed only on the training
portion of each outer fold to prevent leakage.

\textbf{Target construction.}
The demand target is binary: a listing is labelled high-demand if its
unavailability rate $r = (90 - \texttt{availability\_90})/90$ exceeds the
75th percentile computed on the training fold (positive-class rate $= 0.25$).
We exclude \texttt{estimated\_occupancy\_l365d}: Inside Airbnb computes it
from review counts (Pearson $r = 0.638$ with \texttt{number\_of\_reviews\_ltm}),
creating feature--target leakage when review NLP features are present.
Three listing types are removed before splitting: (1)~long-term rentals
(\texttt{minimum\_nights} $> 90$); (2)~host-closed calendars
(\texttt{has\_availability = f}); (3)~never-reviewed listings
(\texttt{number\_of\_reviews} $= 0$), reducing the dataset from 62{,}650 to
53{,}348 rows. Missing numeric values are replaced with per-column medians
computed on each outer training fold.

\textbf{Logistic Regression (simple).}
L2-penalised logistic regression optimises the cross-entropy loss, yielding a
linear decision boundary with feature standardisation applied internally.
$C = 0.001$ (strong regularisation) was selected in 8 of 10 outer folds,
suggesting the high-dimensional feature space benefits from aggressive weight
shrinkage.

\textbf{CatBoost (medium, NeurIPS 2018).}
Gradient-boosted symmetric trees with ordered boosting \cite{prokhorenkova2018}.
Standard gradient boosting suffers from prediction-shift bias: the gradient for
a sample is computed using model predictions that incorporate that sample's
contribution. CatBoost eliminates this by computing residuals on a
permutation-ordered training subset, yielding unbiased gradients without
held-out folds. Tree depth $= 12$ was selected in all 10 outer folds.
Fixed: 100 iterations, learning rate $= 0.1$.

\textbf{FT-Transformer (complex, NeurIPS 2021).}
Each feature is mapped to a $d$-dimensional embedding via
$\mathbf{t}_i = x_i\mathbf{W}_i + \mathbf{b}_i$ (Feature Tokenizer)
\cite{gorishniy2021}. A learnable \texttt{[CLS]} token is prepended and the
sequence processed by $L$ Pre-LayerNorm Transformer encoder layers with GELU
activations and multi-head self-attention, allowing all feature pairs to interact
at every layer. The \texttt{[CLS]} output feeds a linear head. Implemented from
scratch in PyTorch; run on Colab T4 GPU (${\approx}750$s/fold). Fixed:
$d = 32$, 4 heads, feedforward dim $= 128$, AdamW lr $= 3{\times}10^{-4}$,
15 epochs, patience 5.

\subsection{Cross-Validation and Hyperparameter Tuning}

\textbf{Outer CV.} 10-fold cross-validation ($k = 10$, satisfying $k \geq 10$),
implemented from scratch. All models use identical folds for fair comparison.
The demand-target threshold is recomputed on each outer training fold to prevent
distributional leakage.

\textbf{Inner CV.} Within each outer training fold, hyperparameters are selected
by nested 3-fold CV, also implemented from scratch, using macro-F1 as the inner
criterion. Grid search covers all candidates in Table~\ref{tab:hpgrid}.

\textbf{Model complexity and performance.}
CatBoost outperforms both Logistic Regression and FT-Transformer:
$\Delta\text{AUC} = +0.047$ over Logistic and $+0.045$ over FT-Transformer
on base features. The confusion matrix (Table~\ref{tab:cm}) reveals why
accuracy is an insensitive criterion here: with 75\% negative samples, a
classifier predicting all negatives achieves 75\% accuracy. CatBoost achieves
high specificity (0.985 --- few false positives) but moderate recall (0.643
--- misses 36\% of high-demand listings), reflecting a class-imbalance trade-off
that gradient-boosted trees handle better through their non-linear splits. The
macro-recall gap between CatBoost (0.814) and Logistic Regression (0.800)
captures this: CatBoost's deeper trees identify more true high-demand listings.

Contrary to expectation, FT-Transformer ($\text{AUC} = 0.856$) matches Logistic
Regression ($\text{AUC} = 0.854$) despite far greater model complexity. The
encoder-layer hyperparameter showed no consistent winner (4, 6, and 8 each
selected 3--4 times), confirming the model is depth-insensitive on this dataset.
This is consistent with Gorishniy et al.\ \cite{gorishniy2021}, who note
tree-based models remain competitive on tabular data: attention over 97
features provides no advantage when demand is dominated by a small number of
strong structured signals.

\textbf{Effect of NLP-engineered text features.}
Adding 33 NLP features produces no measurable improvement for any model:
$\Delta\text{AUC} = +0.001$ (Logistic), $-0.003$ (CatBoost), $-0.001$
(FT-Transformer) --- all within one standard error. VADER sentiment and
Flesch-Kincaid readability are proxies for listing quality already well-captured
by review scores, superhost status, and host tenure in the base feature set.
At the 90-day horizon, demand is governed by price, location, and capacity
rather than host communication style. This negative result is informative:
richer NLP representations (e.g.\ SBERT embeddings) are unlikely to improve
short-stay demand prediction unless structured quality signals are unavailable.

\textbf{Alternatives considered.}
TabNet \cite{arik2021} was initially selected as the complex model but does not
qualify under the required venues (NeurIPS/ICML/ICLR/JMLR/TMLR); replaced by
FT-Transformer \cite{gorishniy2021}. LightGBM (NeurIPS 2017) was considered but
falls in the same model class as CatBoost, violating the different-complexity
tier requirement. \texttt{estimated\_occupancy\_l365d} was rejected as a target
due to feature--target leakage ($r = 0.638$ with review counts).

%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This study compared Logistic Regression, CatBoost (NeurIPS 2018), and
FT-Transformer (NeurIPS 2021) for predicting high-demand Airbnb listings across
53{,}348 Australian properties, with and without NLP-engineered text features.
Two main findings emerge. First, model architecture dominates: CatBoost achieves
ROC-AUC $= 0.901$, outperforming both Logistic Regression ($0.854$) and
FT-Transformer ($0.856$) by $\Delta\text{AUC} \approx 0.047$, demonstrating
that short-stay demand is governed by non-linear structural interactions that
gradient-boosted trees handle best. Second, NLP text features provide no
measurable lift for any model ($|\Delta\text{AUC}| \leq 0.003$), indicating
VADER sentiment and Flesch-Kincaid readability are redundant given the structured
quality signals present in base features. Practitioners seeking to improve
demand prediction should prioritise richer structural features and well-tuned
gradient-boosted models over text feature engineering.
